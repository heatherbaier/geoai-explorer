What is machine learning?

Before diving into specific algorithms, let's build an honest mental model of what ML is actually doing — and what it isn't.

Section 1

What machine learning actually does

Machine learning is not magic, and it's not a brain. It's a process for automatically finding patterns in data — patterns that would take a human too long to find by hand, or that are too complex to write as explicit rules.

Traditional programming

You write the rules

A human expert looks at the problem and writes explicit if/then rules. "If a parcel is within 100m of a stream AND has poor drainage → flag as high risk."

This works when the rules are simple and well-understood. It breaks down when the problem is complex, the rules interact in non-obvious ways, or the rules change over time.

Machine learning

The data writes the rules

Instead of writing rules yourself, you provide labeled examples — inputs paired with correct outputs. The algorithm finds the pattern that maps inputs to outputs across all your examples.

The rules still exist — they're just extracted from data automatically rather than written by hand. This is the fundamental shift ML introduces.

Where it's used in GIS

Common use cases

Land cover classification — given satellite imagery, predict whether each pixel is forest, urban, water, or cropland. Features are pixel band values; labels come from field surveys or manual annotation.

Species distribution modeling — given environmental variables (elevation, temperature, precipitation), predict where a species is likely to occur. Labels are presence/absence records from field observations.

Urban heat prediction — given neighborhood features (canopy, impervious surface, building density), predict surface temperature anomaly. Labels come from thermal satellite measurements.

Section 2

Supervised vs unsupervised learning

The single biggest distinction in ML is whether your training data has labels. This changes everything — the algorithm, the output, and what you can even ask the model to do.

Supervised learning

Training data has labels — correct answers the model learns to reproduce. The model learns a mapping from inputs to outputs by comparing its predictions to the known correct answers and adjusting.

  • You need labeled examples (expensive to collect)
  • Output is a prediction: a class or a number
  • You can evaluate it objectively — right or wrong
  • Examples: flood risk (YES/NO), UHI intensity (high/medium/low), species presence/absence

Unsupervised learning

Training data has no labels — no correct answers. The model finds structure in the inputs on its own: groups of similar examples, patterns, or compressed representations.

  • No labels needed — just features
  • Output is structure: clusters, components, anomalies
  • Harder to evaluate — "correct" isn't well defined
  • Examples: grouping neighborhoods by land use, detecting anomalous sensor readings, compressing satellite imagery
Same data, different questions
Supervised question: "Given these 15 parcels with known flood outcomes, build a model that predicts flood risk for new parcels."

The algorithm knows what it's trying to predict. Every update to the model is guided by comparing predictions to known labels.
Unsupervised question: "Given these 15 parcels with no labels at all, find groups of similar parcels."

The algorithm has no target to aim for. It finds structure based purely on which parcels have similar feature values — and you interpret what those groups mean afterward.

Raw data — everything you have about the problem before any processing. Field measurements, sensor readings, survey responses, satellite pixel values, administrative records. You are the labeler

What is environmental vulnerability? A neighborhood's exposure to environmental hazards combined with its capacity to cope — considering access to green space, healthcare, income, and air quality. There's no single universally agreed-upon definition, which is part of the point.
10 neighborhoods — label each one
What you're doing — and why it matters

You just did the hardest part of supervised machine learning: creating the labels. Every supervised model needs someone to do exactly this — look at examples and decide what the correct output is.

Notice how some neighborhoods felt obvious, and some were genuinely difficult. The difficult ones — where reasonable people disagree — are called boundary cases. They're where models also struggle, because the training signal itself is ambiguous.

When you trained a model on labels you created, you're also encoding your judgment into it. A different labeler might produce slightly different labels — and a slightly different model. This is why label quality and labeler consistency matter enormously.

Feature guide
Pop. density — people per km². Higher density can amplify exposure to hazards.
Median income — USD/year. Lower income correlates with less capacity to cope with environmental stress.
Tree cover % — fraction of neighborhood with canopy. Reduces heat exposure and air pollution.
Dist. to hospital — km to nearest emergency facility. Greater distance = reduced adaptive capacity.
Air quality index — 0–500 scale. Higher = worse. Values above 100 are considered unhealthy for sensitive groups.

Section 5

Key takeaways

01

ML learns rules from data

You don't program the rules — you provide labeled examples and the algorithm finds the rules. The model is the compressed representation of those patterns.

02

Everything is a number

A model cannot read text, images, or geographic features directly. Everything — pixels, categories, coordinates — must be translated into a numeric feature vector before the model can process it.

03

Supervised needs labels

Labels are the most expensive part of supervised learning. Collecting them requires domain expertise, field work, or historical records. The quality of your labels sets a ceiling on model performance.
