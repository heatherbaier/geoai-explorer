K-means

K-Means Clustering

Grouping Phoenix neighborhoods by socioeconomic and environmental profile — without any labels.

Your scenario

You're a GIS analyst at the City of Phoenix Office of Community Planning. Your team has been asked to identify which neighborhoods share similar socioeconomic and environmental conditions — not to predict a specific outcome, but to find natural groupings that could inform where to target resources, outreach, or further study.

You have five variables measured for 40 Phoenix-area neighborhoods: median household income, tree canopy coverage, population density, distance to the nearest park, and PM2.5 air pollution. Nobody has labeled these neighborhoods — there's no "correct" answer. Your job: use K-means to find the structure in the data, then interpret what the clusters actually represent.

What K-means does

Finding groups without labels

K-means is an unsupervised algorithm — there are no labels, no right answers, no loss function to minimize. Instead it asks: given K groups, how should we divide these points so that each group is as tight (internally similar) as possible?

The result is K cluster assignments — every neighborhood belongs to exactly one cluster — plus a centroid (the average position) for each cluster. The algorithm finds this structure without ever being told what the groups should mean.

How it works

Three steps, repeated

1. Initialize — place K centroids somewhere in the feature space. Where you start matters.

2. Assign — each point joins the cluster whose centroid is closest (by Euclidean distance in feature space).

3. Update — each centroid moves to the mean position of all points currently assigned to it.

Repeat steps 2 and 3 until the assignments stop changing — that's convergence. Usually takes 10–20 iterations.

The hard question

How many clusters?

K-means requires you to choose K before running. Unlike supervised learning, there's no accuracy score to optimize — so how do you know if K=3 is better than K=4?

One common approach is the elbow method: run K-means for K=1 through 8 and plot the total within-cluster variance (inertia). Where the curve bends sharply — the "elbow" — is often a good K. But there's no single right answer, and domain knowledge matters as much as the math.

K-means limitations

Assumes spherical clusters. K-means works well when clusters are roughly round and similarly sized. Elongated or non-convex shapes get split incorrectly.

Sensitive to initialization. Different random starting centroids can produce different final clusters. Running multiple times and keeping the best result (K-means++) helps.

You interpret the clusters. The algorithm assigns numbers — it doesn't tell you what Cluster 2 means. That interpretation step requires domain knowledge, and it's where GIS expertise matters most.
