Random Forest

Predicting flood risk across Houston using an ensemble of decision trees.

Your scenario

It's August in Houston. Harris County Emergency Management has just finished reviewing flood damage from the last storm cycle, and your team has been asked to build a more reliable risk model than the single decision tree you built last week — one that won't fall apart when a few training parcels change.

Your colleague suggests a random forest: instead of one tree trained on all the data, build many trees, each on a different random subsample, then let them vote. Your job today is to understand why that works — by reading three trees with different first splits, tracing five test parcels through each one by hand, and watching the ensemble majority vote outperform any single tree.

Background

Why does Houston flood?

Houston sits on a flat coastal plain with clay-heavy soils that drain poorly, bisected by a network of bayous — Buffalo Bayou, Brays Bayou, White Oak Bayou — that overflow during heavy rainfall. The city is also one of the most impervious urban surfaces in the US: decades of low-density sprawl replaced native prairie with concrete, dramatically reducing infiltration.

Parcels close to bayou channels, with high impervious cover, flat grades, and poor soil drainage face compounding risk — each factor amplifies the others. The features in this dataset were chosen to capture exactly that interaction.

Model inputs

Why these four features?

  • Distance to stream (m) — Proximity to Buffalo Bayou or Brays Bayou is the dominant risk factor. Parcels within 75m face direct inundation during moderate events; beyond 200m risk drops sharply for most storm types.
  • Impervious surface (%) — Houston's bayou corridors are lined with high-impervious neighborhoods (Meyerland, Westbury, Montrose). Paved surfaces prevent infiltration and accelerate runoff directly into channels.
  • Slope (°) — The coastal plain is nearly flat. Even a 5–8° slope substantially improves drainage. Very flat parcels (<3°) pond water in place regardless of soil type.
  • Drainage class — Houston's heavy clay soils are notoriously poor draining. Soil drainage class captures infiltration capacity independently of slope and pavement cover.
The model

Why use a random forest?

A single decision tree is brittle — its structure can shift dramatically if a few training parcels change. This matters in flood risk modeling, where ground-truth labels (past flood events) are sparse and noisy.

A random forest fixes this by training many trees on different random subsamples of the data, each also seeing a random subset of features. No single tree sees the full picture — but the errors each tree makes are different and largely independent. When the trees vote, idiosyncratic errors get outvoted, and only systematic patterns survive.

In sklearn a real forest uses 100–500 trees. Here you'll work with 3 — small enough to read every split, large enough to see the ensemble effect when the trees disagree.

key concept
Why does using less data per tree make the forest more accurate?

Each tree makes different errors because it saw different training data. When you aggregate many imperfect trees, errors that are idiosyncratic to one tree get outvoted. Only errors that are systematic across all trees survive — and those reflect genuine difficulty in the data, not noise.
