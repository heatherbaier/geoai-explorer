Decision Tree

Predicting flood risk across 15 parcels using GIS-derived features.

Your scenario

You're a GIS analyst at a county planning office. Last spring's storms flooded dozens of properties, and your team has been asked to build a model that can flag high-risk parcels before next season — so emergency managers can pre-position resources and planners can prioritize stormwater upgrades.

You have 15 labeled parcels where past flood events have been confirmed or ruled out. Each parcel is described by four features measurable directly from GIS layers — no historical insurance data required. Your job: build a decision tree by hand, choosing every split yourself, so you understand exactly what rules the model is learning before you trust it on new data.

Background

What drives flood risk?

Flood risk is the product of water accumulation and a parcel's ability to drain or resist it. Proximity to a stream determines exposure — parcels close to a channel are first to inundate when it overflows. But exposure alone doesn't determine damage: a well-drained parcel on a steep slope sheds water quickly, while a flat, paved parcel with poor soils holds it.

The four features in this dataset each capture a different piece of that picture — together they give a reasonably complete picture of risk from GIS layers alone, without needing any flood insurance or claims history.

Model inputs

Why these four features?

  • Distance to stream (m) — The single strongest predictor. Parcels within 50–100m of a channel face direct inundation when water levels rise. Beyond ~200m, risk drops sharply for most storm events.
  • Impervious surface (%) — Roads, rooftops, and parking lots prevent infiltration. High impervious cover means runoff accumulates quickly and has nowhere to go — raising downstream risk and local ponding.
  • Slope (°) — Steep parcels shed water downhill. Flat parcels let it pool. A low slope combined with poor drainage is a reliable risk indicator even far from streams.
  • Drainage class — Soil drainage capacity (poor / moderate / good) captures infiltration potential. Poor-draining soils saturate quickly, turning even modest rainfall into standing water.
The model

Why use a decision tree?

A decision tree is one of the most interpretable models in machine learning — it produces explicit if/then rules that a planner or emergency manager can read and verify. "If distance to stream ≤ 75m AND drainage class is poor → HIGH RISK" is a rule you can defend in a public meeting.

Trees are also well-suited to mixed feature types. This dataset has both numeric features (distance, slope, impervious %) and a categorical one (drainage class) — a decision tree handles both natively without any special encoding.

The key concept you'll practice here is Gini impurity: a measure of how mixed the classes are at each node. A good split drives impurity toward zero on both sides — one branch mostly HIGH RISK, the other mostly LOW RISK.
